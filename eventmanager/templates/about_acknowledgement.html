Acknowledgement of Country

Understanding a meaningful practice for reconciliation

What is an Acknowledgement of Country?

An Acknowledgement of Country is a formal recognition of the Traditional Custodians and their continuing connection to the land where an event is held. It is an important practice that:

  • Recognizes Indigenous peoples' enduring connection to Country
  • Demonstrates respect and cultural awareness
  • Plays an important role in reconciliation and truth-telling
  • Acknowledges that events occur on unceded or stolen land in most cases
Acknowledgement vs. Welcome to Country

Acknowledgement of Country: A statement made by non-Indigenous people, recognizing Traditional Custodians

Welcome to Country: A ceremonial welcome performed by an Indigenous person from the Traditional Custodians of that Country. This is a privilege, not a right, and should be formally invited.

Types of Acknowledgement Available
No Acknowledgement

Your event will not include an acknowledgement.

Generic Acknowledgement

A general, non-specific acknowledgement of Traditional Custodians. Example:

"We acknowledge the Traditional Custodians of the land on which this event is held and pay our respects to their Elders past, present, and emerging."
Enhanced Acknowledgement

A specific acknowledgement that names the Traditional Custodians of the event's location. This requires research and demonstrates greater cultural respect.

Why Acknowledgement Matters
  • Recognition: It acknowledges the continuing connection of Indigenous peoples to Country
  • Respect: It shows respect for Indigenous cultures and perspectives
  • Reconciliation: It is a step towards truth-telling and healing
  • Responsibility: Event organizers take responsibility for acknowledging the land's history

Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Being Tokenistic: Don't use acknowledgement as a "tick box" exercise. It should be meaningful.
  • Inaccurate Names: Always verify the correct names of Traditional Custodians. Mispronunciation or incorrect names are disrespectful.
  • Vague Language: Avoid generic "Indigenous peoples" when you can name specific groups.
  • No Follow-up: An acknowledgement should be backed by genuine action toward reconciliation.
  • Performative without Action: If you're acknowledging Traditional Custodians, consider how you can support them (e.g., hiring Indigenous speakers, supporting Indigenous causes).

Reliable Resources for Research
  • AIATSIS (Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies): Provides comprehensive information on Indigenous groups and their Countries
  • Local Council/Shire Websites: Often have information about Traditional Custodians
  • National Native Title Tribunal: Searchable database of Native Title claims
  • UNESCO - Intangible Cultural Heritage: Information on Indigenous knowledge and practices
  • Contact Local Indigenous Communities: When possible, reach out directly for guidance
<
Tips for Creating a Meaningful Acknowledgement
  1. Research: Spend time learning about the Traditional Custodians of your location
  2. Be Specific: Name the specific group(s) if possible
  3. Use Correct Pronunciation: Learn how to pronounce names correctly
  4. Show Connection to Land: Mention the specific Country or place name if known
  5. Demonstrate Respect: Use respectful language and acknowledge their ongoing role
  6. Back It Up: Accompany acknowledgement with action (hiring, donations, learning opportunities)
  7. Continuously Learn: Reconciliation is an ongoing journey of learning
Note: This information is provided as an educational resource for university students. Always do your own research and consult with local Indigenous communities for guidance specific to your location.
